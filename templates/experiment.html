Background:

Influenza is a virus that affects the upper respiratory tract. Common in cold and dry weather, the virus has a 'season'. There are 3 types of the virus: type A, type B, and type C. Type A can spread quickly and in many mammals while type B and C only affect humans. Type C does no real damage as it causes a mild infection, while type B can be devastating. Type A and B both have sub-categories based on the two main proteins of the virus: hemagglutinin and neuraminidase (H and N). The different evolution on the viruses rely on changes made to these proteins. Hemagglutinin are used in attaching the virus onto a cell, while the neuraminidase is used to spread the infection. Once the virus attaches to the host cell, the genetic material of the virus enters the cell and takes over the organelles of the cell to make more flu viruses. Flu vaccines contain three different strands of these H and N proteins usually and each one helps prevent the flu from these strands. Scientists use a technique called sequence alignment to try to get the best match possible for the yearly vaccine. BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool) is an online based bioinformatics tool for aligning sequences. For this experiment, I will be using different flu seasons from 2000-2007

Hypothesis:

If the protein alignment of the flu vaccine were similar to the protein alignment of a non-matching flu virus, then the effectiveness of the flu vaccine increases

Materials:

A Computer with internet

Procedure:

1) Using the chart from Science Buddies determine the strains found in the wild that are not in the vaccine for that year. (They are bolded on the chart)
2) Record the names of each strain, and their type. Also record the matching type found in the vaccine. (Leave room for accession number, will get later)
3) For each strain, use the GenBank database to find the protein sequences. Beside the search bar at the top of the page, select protein from the dropdown menu and insert the name of the flu strain in the search box and click go.
4) Look for a search result that says “hemagglutinin [*virus name*]” and “neuraminidase [*virus name*]” Make sure they do NOT have “partial” in the name! Click on the search result you want. Listed will be an Accession number. Record that number with the name and the type of flu strain for both the H and N proteins. If you cannot find the sequence for a protein, skip it.
5) Using BLAST, insert the accession numbers of the H protein from the subtype in the wild in one box and the number of the subtype in the vaccine, then click the "Blast!" button.
6) A page will load and there will be a percentage in a chart that says ident over it. This is the amount of amino acids that match up in a sequence in the two proteins. Record this percentage in the chart.
7) Repeat steps 5 and 6 for all the flu virus wild-type vs vaccines that are the same type and in the same season.
8) Repeat steps 5, 6 and 7 for the N proteins

Analysis:

Many of the viruses had a similar structure and amino acid sequences on both proteins. The lowest percentage of match was 93% with the Malaysia virus and the Florida Virus with the H protein. The highest percentage of match was 99% in the H protein between Wisconsin v California and in the N protein, between Wisconsin and California. The comparison between Wisconsin v California, Fujian v Moscow and Panama v Moscow are the only results we can reliably predict the effectiveness.

Conclusion:

Due to there being only three comparisons with both the H and N proteins, and there being no way to actually measure effectiveness, I have to predict the effectiveness. From the data collected, I am concluding that the experiment supports the hypothesis. With the percentage of the two proteins of the Wisconsin and California virus both being 99%, this vaccine would be very effective against the strain. This means that there are limited amount of mutations between the proteins and that they are almost identical, so the immune system should have no problem protecting the body from the virus. The flu vaccine containing the Moscow virus and the season with Panama wildtype, the percentage between the proteins are 98% which I conclude would be effective against the wildtypes. The other virus percents that I collected do not have both proteins to compare so I cannot conclude whether or not the vaccine would be affected.
