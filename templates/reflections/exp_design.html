Experimental Design to Test a Hypothesis:
CO2 Experimental Inquiry

Expaination:

The artifact that represents my best example of an experimental design to test a hypothesis is the CO2 Experimental Inquiry lab. In this lab, we were working on the scientific process and the general lab safety procedures and precautions. Everyone had a partner and we had to design a lab to test the CO2 output before and after different tasks. Before the tasks, we would fill a beaker with water and Bromthymol Blue (an indicator), then using a straw, breathe a full breath into the beaker. The CO2 that was coming from the breath would mix with the water and make a weak carbonic acid. We would record the color the BTB changed as this tells us relatively how much CO2 was produced. We would then do our task, then repeat the steps to get the BTB color. My groups task was playing a piece of music on a trumpet at different tempos to see if out CO2 output would be changed. Our hypothesis was that the faster the tempo, the more CO2 the person would produce after playing. Our results showed that playing the trumpet did increase the rate of CO2 production, but differing tempos played had little effect on the rate.

I chose this artifact as the best example because all my other inquiry labs this semester had a flaw in them or the did not work as expected, as in we had complications and modified the experiment halfway through. This lab also gave me a general idea how to design my own experiments and carry them out by myself. This was an incredibly important skill to learn as many labs also had an inquiry. Although we had not started on actual biology yet, this was a way to get our feet wet with cellular respiration also as it had to deal with CO2 output rates.
